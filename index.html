CHARACTERS IN NOLI ME TANGERE
Padre Damaso
  • Damaso is a friar belonging to the Catholic Church.
  • He is depicted as an individual who leverages his authority for personal gain, accumulating riches and influence while exploiting the Filipino populace.
  • Padre Dámaso's character is characterized by hypocrisy in his religious convictions. Despite his role as a representative of the Catholic Church, his actions frequently contradict the teachings of the faith.
Juan Crisóstomo Ibarra y Magsalin
  • Crisostomo Ibarra is the main character, and he's a well-educated young man who comes back to the Philippines after studying in Europe. He has a strong desire to make things better in his country.
  • He's a caring and determined person, and he's known for wanting to make things fair for the Filipino people who are suffering because of the Spanish rule.
  • As the story goes on, he changes because of the difficulties he faces. He becomes more willing to take bold actions to bring justice and improvement to his homeland.
Don Rafael Ibarra
  • The father of Crisostomo Ibarra, unjustly imprisoned, motivating his son's quest for justice.
  • He symbolizes the injustices faced by the Filipino elite under Spanish rule.
  • Don Rafael advocated for change through peaceful means.
Don Santíago de los Santos
  • A wealthy Filipino businessman caught between loyalty to Spanish authorities and his Filipino heritage.
  • Reflects the complexities and conflicts faced by the Filipino elite.
  • Hosts social gatherings that reveal the hypocrisy and superficiality of society.
Padre Salvi
  • A cunning and manipulative friar who represents the corrupt Catholic Church.
  • An antagonist who exploits his position for personal gain.
  • Engages in deceitful actions to maintain power and control.
Narcisa (Sisa)
  • Sisa is the mother of Basilio and Crispin.
  • Sisa has been subjected to abuse done by both the Civil Authorities and her husband, Pedro.
  • She is depicted as an impoverished vulnerable woman who faces challenges in meeting her financial obligations.
  • She serves as a symbol for the challenges faced by women within the context of colonial society, wherein they frequently experienced the weight of subordination and inequality.
Don Tiburcio de Espadaña
  • Don Tiburcio de Espadaña is characterized by his comically exaggerated claims of European heritage, which he uses to elevate his social status.
  • He is known for his questionable and ineffective medical profession, emphasizing the theme of deceit and incompetence in the novel.
  • Don Tiburcio is the abusive husband of Donya Consolacion, and their turbulent relationship sheds light on domestic abuse within the narrative.
Don Anastacio or Pilosopo Tasyo
  • Tasyo is a highly educated philosopher, known for his profound intellectual pursuits.
  • His eccentric behavior and unconventional ideas set him apart, making him a notable figure in the story.
  • Tasyo's character serves as a profound embodiment of the theme of intellectual resistance to the oppressive colonial regime, showcasing the power of critical thought and non-conformist beliefs in challenging the status quo.
Basilio
  • Basilio is one of the young brothers who initially worked as a servant in the parish.
  • He is characterized by his determination and resourcefulness, which come to the forefront after the separation from Crispin.
  • Basilio's character evolves as he becomes more deeply involved in the story's events, seeking justice for the injustices he witnesses and ultimately playing a crucial role in the novel's resolution.
Donya Consolacion
  • In Jose Rizal's "Noli Me Tangere," the character Donya Consolacion, who is portrayed as a harsh and domineering figure, also experiences mistreatment and abuse at the hands of her husband, Alperes. This dynamic adds complexity to the novel's exploration of power and relationships.
  • While Donya Consolacion may be cruel and oppressive toward her servants, she, too, becomes a victim within her own domestic sphere. Her husband, Alperes, is depicted as a drunkard who often abuses her physically. This aspect of their relationship serves to underscore the pervasive nature of abuse and the cyclical nature of mistreatment that can occur in hierarchical and authoritarian systems.
  • Rizal's portrayal of Donya Consolacion as both an oppressor and a victim adds depth to her character and highlights the broader themes of oppression, abuse, and the corrosive impact of the colonial society depicted in the novel. It reminds readers that individuals trapped in oppressive systems can also suffer themselves, perpetuating a cycle of violence and control.
Crispin
  • Crispin is one of the young brothers who worked as a servant in the parish.
  • He is portrayed as timid and submissive, suffering mistreatment and abuse.
  • Crispin's character plays a significant role in the novel when he is falsely accused of theft, which results in further suffering and becomes a pivotal point in the storyline.
